"Art of Lifting Heavy"

by Michael Parker




Lifting heavy isn't just about the amount you can move. You see, lifting heavy is more a form of art than you'd think, it can cause immediate emotional reactions just as a painting or sculpture. When people see someone complete a ~500 kg (1102 lb) deadlift a natural reaction of just aw.

It takes years to master some of these lifts such as squats, deadlifts, or cleans. Most beginning artists aren't able to paint master pieces over night just as a beginner lifter more than likely wouldn't be able to correctly do a clean and jerk for the first time.

There is a shift that happens in lifting when you decide to go heavy. Form becomes the most important thing, without it you will more than likely injure yourself or fail the lift.

You know I can remember my first time squatting heavy, I was 145lbs at the time attempting a 225lb squat at Western Oregon University's gym.

Thing is to someone of my frame that's a big deal. Bar loaded, I take my position, my feet space themselves out into the perfect shoulder width and my hands somehow have a grip on the bar death couldn't even handle. My body was ready but my mind, mentally I had a block.


With a swift slap to my ass my partner yells, "Form looks great, let's go. You got this!". I snap out of it and unrack while taking in a massive breath. Shacking all over I engage every muscle fiber, step back, and go ass to grass.

To me the weight felt unbearable and when I hit the bottom my legs wanted to give out and explode. Everyone's eyes were on me at this point, and that's when my pride kicked in and my body found the strength. Face super red because I forgot to let my breath out on the way up, I successfully rack and hear cheering while I proceed to receive more slaps to my ass in victory.


That was the last day of a 60 day training program doing a classic routine, The 5 X 5. I hit all my goals and more. I started out 135 lbs and ended 145 lbs with a ~135 lb overall average increase to my max weight per lift. My starting & ending stats I've posted below:

Now let's dive into how the 5 x 5 routine works. You have 2 sets of workout cards: A & B

5 x 5 Workout A
Squats Sets: 5 Reps: 5 Weight: 45 lbs
Bench Sets: 5 Reps: 5 Weight: 45 lbs
Landmine Press Sets: 5 Reps: 5 Weight: 45 lbs

5 x 5 Workout B
Squats Sets: 5 Reps: 5 Weight: 45 lbs
Barbell Rows Sets: 5 Reps: 5 Weight: 45 lbs
Deadlift Sets: 1 Reps: 5 Weight: 65 lbs

This is an example of a beginner lifters card set. Each week for each exercise you will increase the weight depending on the exercise by 5 or 10 lbs. It is most common for people with squats and deadlift to go up 10 lbs each week and barbell rows, landmine press, and bench by 5 lbs.

These lifts are specific and should be the ones to use as they are compound movements. Compound movements are core for every lifter to learn, master and have in their regiment of lifts.

You'll notice deadlift is the only lift with 1 set. It can be very challenging for a beginner lifter to squat and deadlift in the same day while each week increasing weight.

The landmine press can be replaced with overhead press. Which the overhead press is usually the standard for this routine; however, if you're someone with a shoulder injury or diagnosed problem than I'd suggest the landmine press. The same muscles will be worked but the movement is safer on our rotator cuffs.



So here is the break down for your weeks during this routine. Lifting 3 days a week, alternating workout cards A -> B -> A -> B, and with rest days in between and weekends off.

Trust me the rest is needed, especially when you start getting into the later weeks. When you lift heavy the body feels it and adapts to the stresses. That time the body is adapting it will demand the energy (calorie intake goes up) and rest to repair tissue.

Muscle and bone density occur due to the progressive weight increase each week so, feed the beast and get proper rest. When we work our muscle fibers out we create micro tears and only during rest do we actually increase the size of our muscle fibers.

Monday Tuesday Wednesday Thursday Friday
Workout B Rest day Workout A Rest day Workout B

Week 2
Monday Tuesday Wednesday Thursday Friday
Workout A Rest day Workout B Rest day Workout A
